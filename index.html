The Panama Canal

Fast Definition:
A canal (small river) made in Panama to give shipping freights a way to cut through latin america. It skips the long trip around South America.

The beginning of the canal

The French first attempted at building the canal. 9 years and 20,000 people later, the attempt stopped. But, the US still wanted to build the canal.

President Teddy Rosevelt dispatched warships to Panama city. They fought for Panamanian independence against Colombia. Panama declared independence November 3, 1903.

The US paid Panama $10 million and a annual annunity payment of $250,000 for a 10 mile wide strip of land in Panama.

The building of the canal

Beginning in 1907, US citizens blasted through mountain. It was a hot climate, but the threat of yellow fever and malaria was diminished by Walter Reed and William Gorgas.

In 1914, the US finished building the canal at a approximate cost of $345 million.

The effect of the canal today

The canal, although built more than 100 years ago, still accounts for 10% of all US shipping.

If something were to happen to the canal, there would be a slow down in goods traded between countries

But, newer ships might not be able to cross the canal in 50 or so years. The size of the locks in the canal are only so big to fit ships.
